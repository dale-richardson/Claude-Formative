D295 Learning Resource  ·  Formative Assessment

56 Examples of
Formative Assessment

Practical strategies teachers can use in any educational setting — from analog classics to current digital tools — to inform instruction and give meaningful feedback.

Originally curated by David Wees, New Visions for Public Schools  ·  Updated for D295 2025–26
Definition

A formative assessment is a tool teachers use to give feedback to students and/or guide their instruction. It is not included in a student grade, nor should it be used to judge a teacher's performance — those would be summative assessments.

Writing-Based Strategies

Reflect & Articulate
01

3× Summarization

Ask students to write three summaries of the same concept: 10–15 words, 30–50 words, and 75–100 words. Different lengths demand different levels of precision and understanding.

writing
02

Write It Down

Students write an explanation of what they understand. Read these to inform your instruction; write comments or discuss them to give individual feedback.

writing
03

Exit Slip

Hand out a short quiz or 2–3 questions; students submit them as they leave. A classic snapshot of whole-class understanding in under five minutes.

writing
04

3-2-1 Reflection

Students write 3 big ideas from the lesson, 2 insights or reflective comments, and 1 remaining question. Great as an exit slip alternative.

writing
05

Two Roses and a Thorn

Students name two things they valued about a chapter or lesson, and one thing they didn't like or still have a question about. Works as a wrap-up or exit ticket.

writing
06

Postcard

Students write a postcard as one historical figure to another, describing a historical event. Forces concise, perspective-taking writing.

writing
07

3 Things

List 3 things a fellow student might misunderstand about the topic — or 3 things you want to know more about. Quick and thought-provoking.

writing
08

Comments on Student Work

Write descriptive comments on student work helping them see how to improve, or what they've done effectively. Prioritize comments over grades for formative purposes.

writing
09

Self-Assessment

After finishing a writing assignment, students evaluate themselves using the same rubric you use. Then discuss their self-evaluation in conference.

writing
10

Answer the Essential Question

Can students answer the lesson's essential question — verbally or in writing? A direct gauge of whether learning goals were met.

writing
11

Make Predictions

Students predict an experiment or demo outcome and explain their reasoning. After the demo, discuss why predictions were right or wrong. Base grades on final conclusions, not predictions.

writing
12

Metacognition Table

At the end of class, students complete a table: What did we do? Why? What did I learn? How can I apply it? What questions remain?

writing

Quick-Check Strategies

Real-Time Pulse
13

Thumbs Up / Middle / Down

Ask if students understand. Thumbs up = got it; sideways = unsure; down = lost. Ancient Rome's version used a closed fist vs. open hand.

quick
14

Fingers Up (1–5)

1 = I need a lot of help; 5 = I really get this. Students hold up fingers so you can scan the room at a glance.

quick
15

Quick Nod

Students nod yes or no to signal understanding. Can also be done digitally with Poll Everywhere or a Google Form for anonymity.

quick
16

Red / Green Card

Students hold an index card (red on one side, green on the other). Green = following along; red = need clarification. Far more efficient than raised hands.

quick
17

Traffic Lights

Green = I know this; Yellow = partly; Red = I don't know this. Use before, during, and after a topic with highlighters, colored cups, or laminated rings.

quick
18

Watch Body Language

Pay careful attention to students' nonverbal cues — confusion, disengagement, and "aha" moments often show before students raise their hands.

quick
19

Raised Hands

When asking questions, note who raises their hand (give sufficient wait time). Track patterns over time to identify who may be disengaged or struggling.

quick
20

Cold-Calling

Use a procedure (e.g., popsicle sticks, random selector) to call on all students equitably — including those who might not otherwise volunteer.

quick
21

Quizzes

Short quizzes — teacher-graded or student-graded — used to guide instruction, not as high-stakes grades. Frequency matters more than length.

quick
22

My Favorite No

Students work a warm-up problem on index cards. Sort into yes/no piles; choose your favorite "no" response and analyze it as a class. Normalizes mistakes.

quick
23

Mini-Whiteboards

Each student (or group) uses a mini-whiteboard to show work. You can scan the room or have students hold up boards simultaneously.

quick
24

Hand in, Pass Out

Students answer anonymously on paper, hand in, teacher redistributes randomly for peer grading, then polls the class informally on results.

quick

Visual & Creative Strategies

Show What You Know
25

Doodle It

Students draw what they understand instead of writing it. Great for visual learners and concepts that lend themselves to diagrams or sequences.

visual
26

Visualize / Be the Illustrator

Read a page without showing the illustration. Students create their own visualization for that page, then compare with the actual image.

visual
27

Venn Diagram

Students compare and contrast two concepts, events, characters, or ideas using a Venn diagram. Reveals depth of understanding in their overlaps and differences.

visual
28

Extension Projects

Creative demonstrations: dioramas, posters, collages, ABC books, or any student-generated project that shows additional understanding of a concept.

visual
29

Photos to Assess Learning

Choose 2–3 photos representing a process. Students write captions for each, followed by a short summary connecting them.

visual
30

Create a Video

Students make short videos or screencasts explaining their reasoning. Watch what they include, omit, and where they stumble to identify gaps.

visual
31

Create Something

Similar to checking for transfer — have students build or make something that requires applying what they've learned. Process reveals understanding.

visual
32

Check for Transfer

Can students apply a concept across contexts? For example: identify climax in a short story, a novel, a movie, and an advertisement.

visual
33

Chalkboard Splash

Multiple students respond to a prompt on the board simultaneously. Produces a real-time display of collective understanding (or misconceptions).

visual

Verbal & Discussion Strategies

Think Aloud
34

Talk to Each Other

Students discuss how they'd accomplish a task or explain an idea. You circulate and listen in — what you overhear shapes your next instructional move.

verbal
35

Think-Pair-Share

Students think individually about a question, pair with a neighbor to discuss, then share with the class. Used with Mazur's ConcepTest for maximum impact.

verbal
36

Listening

Ask students to explain how they know something is true. Listen for misconceptions beneath the surface of an answer that sounds correct.

verbal
37

Ask Clarifying Questions

Probe student thinking with follow-up questions. Record observations on an anticipation template to spot patterns across the class.

verbal
38

Text Rendering

Students read a text, then each share a sentence → phrase → single word they found most significant. Groups compare which words and phrases bubbled up.

verbal
39

Running Records

Assess students reading aloud, tracking what they do and what mistakes they make (including self-corrections). Valuable for reading development insight.

verbal
40

Anticipate Responses

Before class: do the work yourself in 2+ ways and anticipate student responses. During class: circulate and record observations on a template.

verbal

Peer-Based Strategies

Learn Together
41

Partner Quizzes

Students work on the first question together, give each other feedback, then work independently on a new question covering the same concept.

peer
42

Jigsaw Groups

Groups become experts on different text sections, then regroup so each new group has one expert per section. Experts share; everyone synthesizes.

peer
43

Teach Younger Kids

Students tutor younger students in basic concepts. Check in with both groups afterward — the quality of tutoring reveals how solidly older students understand the material.

peer
44

Hand Thermometer

After a student shares work, classmates raise hands to varying heights to give peer feedback: all the way up = excellent; half = okay; just above desk = needs work.

peer
45

Rotate Groups / Stations

Students rotate through activity stations. In small groups, the teacher assesses and gives feedback — higher-quality interactions than whole-class formats allow.

peer
46

Crowd-Source a Presentation

Students create a shared presentation (like this one) and open it to their learning network for edits. Feedback from peers and teachers becomes the formative data.

peer

Digital & Technology Strategies

⚡ Digital Tools
47

Google Forms

Pose lesson questions in a Google Form. Students who don't participate verbally will often respond digitally. Anonymous options increase honesty.

digital
48

Clickers / Student Response Systems

Students respond individually to questions using clickers or services like Kahoot!, Poll Everywhere, or Mentimeter. Instant whole-class data.

digital
49

Socrative

Students log into your virtual classroom and respond to pre-posted or on-the-spot questions. Real-time data shows individual and class results. (socrative.com)

digital
50

Poll Everywhere

Live polling students can respond to from any device — phone, laptop, or tablet. Results display visually in real time on your projected screen. (polleverywhere.com)

digital
51

Google Docs (Live Feedback)

Have students write in Google Docs. You or a peer observes their writing in real time and gives live comments — immediate feedback without collecting papers.

digital
52

Back Channel (Padlet / Jamboard)

As you teach, students post ideas, questions, and comments to a digital board. Captures quiet students' thinking and lets you address misconceptions mid-lesson.

digital
53

Digital Cork Board (Padlet)

Students collaboratively post closing thoughts, questions, and comments on a shared digital board. Padlet replaced the discontinued Linoit. (padlet.com)

digital
54

Nearpod / Pear Deck

Interactive slides where students respond to embedded questions, drawings, or polls as you present. Every student response is visible in real time to the teacher.

digital
55

Flipgrid / Flip

Students record short video responses to prompts. Teacher and peers can view and respond. Great for checking understanding and building communication skills. (flip.com)

digital
56

Kahoot! / Gimkit / Blooket

Game-based quiz platforms that generate engagement alongside data. Use results to identify concepts needing reteaching — not to produce grades.

digital

📋 How to Use This Resource in D295

For Any Subject All 56 strategies are subject-agnostic. Every discipline can use writing, quick checks, and discussion strategies.
Low-Tech Settings Strategies 01–46 require no technology. Many need only paper, index cards, or whiteboards.
High-Tech Settings Strategies 47–56 leverage digital tools. Most have free educator tiers or district licensing.
Not for Grading Every strategy here is formative — use them to inform your teaching, not to calculate student grades.
Start Small Pick two or three strategies from different categories and test them before expanding your repertoire.
